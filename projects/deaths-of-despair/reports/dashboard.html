Chapter 1 — The Timeline

Three Waves of Death — One Root Cause

The opioid epidemic didn't happen randomly. It was engineered. Purdue Pharma launched OxyContin in 1996 with a fraudulent marketing campaign claiming it was non-addictive. Sales went from $44M to $2.9B in 15 years. When the pills were eventually reformulated in 2010, addicted patients switched to heroin — and then to cheap, lethal fentanyl. Watch the death toll rise in lockstep with pharma revenue.

Sources: CDC NCHS (overdose deaths, age-adjusted per 100k), Purdue Pharma bankruptcy court filings SDNY 2019 (revenue)

The Sackler Family — $10+ Billion in Personal Distributions

While America buried its dead, the Sackler family — owners of Purdue Pharma — paid themselves over $10 billion in personal distributions between 2008 and 2017. They moved money to offshore accounts as lawsuits piled up. In 2007, Purdue pleaded guilty to federal fraud charges and paid $634.5M — one of the largest pharmaceutical settlements at the time. Three top executives paid $34.5M personally. The company continued operating.

The death toll continued to rise.

Chapter 3 — The Machine

Prescriptions Written, Deaths That Followed

At the peak in 2012, 81.3 opioid prescriptions were written for every 100 Americans — enough for every adult in the country to have their own bottle. Pharmaceutical companies lobbied doctors, funded "pain management" organizations, and spread the false message that opioids were safe. The correlation between prescriptions and deaths is not coincidence.

Chapter 4 — The Profits

An $11 Billion Industry Built on Addiction

The U.S. opioid market peaked at $11 billion in annual revenue in 2011–2012. Purdue Pharma alone made $35+ billion from OxyContin. Distributors McKesson, AmerisourceBergen, and Cardinal Health collectively shipped hundreds of billions of pills while regulators looked the other way. The executives knew. Internal emails, now public record, show Purdue tracking addiction rates while explicitly not reporting them to the FDA.

Sources: IQVIA/IMS Health opioid market data (cited in JAMA, NEJM studies); CDC NCHS overdose deaths
Source: DOJ press releases, state AG announcements, court records (public)

The Settlement Numbers Sound Big — But Consider This

The $26 billion settlement paid by the Big Three distributors (McKesson, AmerisourceBergen, Cardinal Health) in 2021 sounds enormous. But those three companies made over $500 billion in combined revenue in 2020 alone. The J&J settlement? They made $82 billion in 2020 revenue. The "largest opioid settlement in history" represents less than 3 months of business. Meanwhile, over half a million Americans are dead.

Chapter 5 — The Economics

Where the Jobs Went, Death Followed

The opioid crisis didn't target randomly. It hit hardest in places where factories had closed, wages had stagnated, and communities had lost their identity. Pharma companies specifically targeted these areas — identifying high-prescribing doctors in economically depressed regions and flooding them with sales reps. The correlation between manufacturing job loss and deaths of despair is not an accident. It's a target.

Data Sources & Methodology

  • Deaths of Despair Rate: Combined drug overdose death rate + suicide death rate, both age-adjusted per 100,000. Source: CDC NCHS datasets jx6g-fdh6, xbxb-epbu, bi63-dtpu via data.cdc.gov Socrata API. Coverage: 1999–2016.
  • Opioid Prescribing Rate: CDC U.S. Opioid Prescribing Rate Maps, published annually. Prescriptions per 100 persons, 2006–2016.
  • Purdue Pharma Revenue: Net revenue from OxyContin sales. Source: Purdue Pharma Chapter 11 bankruptcy court filings (SDNY 2019), DOJ press releases, peer-reviewed studies citing court documents.
  • U.S. Opioid Market: Total U.S. opioid analgesic market revenue. Source: IQVIA (formerly IMS Health) data as cited in JAMA, NEJM, and CDC reports.
  • ARCOS Pill Data: DEA ARCOS opioid pill shipments per capita by state (2010). Source: DEA ARCOS database published by Washington Post investigative team, 2019.
  • Manufacturing Employment: All-Employees: Manufacturing by state (annual average). Source: FRED/BLS State and Area Employment Statistics.
  • Legal Settlements: From DOJ press releases, state attorney general announcements, and public court records.

Limitations: Death rate data covers 1999–2016 (CDC NCHS age-adjusted state rates available through this period for both overdose and suicide). Pharma revenue data for years pre-2007 is based on estimates from court documents; precise figures remain sealed. Correlation does not prove causation — though in this case, court-verified documents explicitly demonstrate intent.
